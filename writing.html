Connor and Diana

24 September 1939

Dear Diana,

Many of my friends were killed today. They died fighting them. They all tried to stop the enemy, but it wasn't enough. I tried too, I swear to God I did, but they terrified me. Every time I looked at them, I thought of you, Diana.

I just couldn't leave you alone. I'm sorry, I was a coward. My family thinks I died alongside my friends; nobody knows that I ran away. I may still be breathing, but inside, I'm as lifeless as that chicken hanging in the butcher's shop. I'm too afraid to come back now. I can't face the families of the soldiers who died saving me while I ran away like a coward. I just can't, Diana.

The purpose of this letter is to confess one last time: I love you. I always have, and I always will. I would've surely married you, but I wasn't brave enough. Today, I'm going to meet my friends again. I don't know if they'll accept me up there, but I can't bear living with myself any longer.

I don't know if this message will ever reach you, but if it does, don't let it weigh on your heart.

Your lover,
Connor

She was magical

her voice was like the pure, cold, icy water falling from a waterfall,
her mere presence was nurturing and positive,
She was like the feeling of wonder and happiness that a child has,
the one person you can trust, even with your eyes pulled out,
She was the one.
But my mom always says god calls the good person as soon as possible,
Although she is not here physically, the echoes of her laughter still keep me awake at night, and the flowery fragrance of her hair still sits here with me
And I don't want to leave them alone ever again.

i've had several funerals

I've had several funerals
some ceremonial
some sad
some even celebratory
But most of them
were quiet, private, and stoic
There were no grand prayers and weeping
It was just someone refusing to recognize me anymore.
I died thousands of times,
from different ways, with diverse personalities and under various names.
And not a single person knows.

Dear Diary

17/05/2026

Sunday

Dear Diary,

I wish to confess, to come clean, and to finally talk about something.

I stutter.

I know exactly what I want to say. I have repeated the same words to myself several times. The people in front of me are eagerly waiting, their faces displaying a mix of emotions. Most are understanding and patient, some are impatient and eager to move on, and a few are laughing. When I see those few, I feel scared and angry—not at them, but at myself, at my unruly tongue and mind.

This has happened countless times. After each instance, my urge to speak, to tell my friends about my day, or even to answer a question, runs far away, to hide in the distant forest. I sometimes feel like one of the stranger creations of the one above. I think a lot, almost all the time, but I often choose not to give it those cruel words.

Maybe that's why I like writing, on paper, on a screen, with my own comfort, no one in my head just shouting at me to speak clearly or quickly.

But recently, I've started to notice the people who were patient, who were understanding, who were with me through those weird pauses, who didn't run away or make fun of me just because I spoke with a short delay.

And I know that I would be stuttering in the future too, and maybe I will always choose the ones who stayed, waited, and were kind enough to listen.

Thank you for being one of the few who stayed.

Every Scar

Every scar on my cold, dry face,
Holds a beautiful memory.
They keep growing.
Finding homes on the flaky skin,
Farming a yellowish fluid.
The colony is yet to arrive,
to my lips, eyes, and heart.

A Melancholic Coincidence:

A constellation forms above the pretty sky,
Each faded star falls in my backyard.
I collect those diverse bodies for show.
The showcase shelf paints a portrait,
Of a familiar face, I saw the day I died.

Miranda

Miranda
Cute, old, and very wise,
with brown, cold, and distant eyes.
She runs out of her small house,
trying to look for a little mouse.

A mouse, like a son,
a pretty, cute one.

Miranda
In a shining hot sun,
Having so much fun.
She walks her path,
like an otter for a bath.

Miranda
finally greets the moon,
hoping to find him soon.
But the mouse is lost,
fleeing as a ghost.

Miranda,
searches for her son,
a pretty, cute one.

What the Monkeys Know

Evening.
They are here, yet again. A family of many—uninvited, yet united. The young ones jump and wrestle the way we used to. A tired mother walks slowly, her baby hugging her upside down. The big ones are stoic and rude. They are familiar.

Tuesday.
I take off my chappal at the gate of the Hanuman Mandir. The continuous jingle of the temple bell, the thump of a worn-out tabla, and a melodious voice fill the air. I join the half-finished Chalisa, reciting along. The priest splashes a handful of cold water from his jug, then comes around and presses a bright orange chandan tika between my eyebrows. When I finally meet him—standing in all of his glory, carrying a mountain—he is, somehow, also kind.

Vrindavan.
A tourist's sunglasses disappear, now resting in the grasp of a small grey fist. Locals come in as translators, informing her about the huge demand for Frooti. She agrees. A man from the crowd throws the Frooti up into the air, and with perfect fairness, a pair of sunglasses comes down.

Morning,
every morning, for almost a month.
A grey and pink hand taps gently at my window. The curtain hides a child. He chatters as if he has something urgent to say. His tongue lolls out as soon as he sees the banana on my desk. I open the window—I always open the window. After a bit of small talk, he gets his breakfast.

One day,
He stopped coming.
My eyes were open, waiting to get up after that delicate knock.

Yesterday.
A big guy—one of the rude ones—on my balcony, in an unusual area. He barely moved, just sat there with his back to me. His back was bulky, with yellow hair poking out, some tangled, drawing an odd image. I watched him long enough that the image started to make sense. Then, abruptly, he moved on. I kept looking, hoping to catch a glimpse of his eyes.

Burial

I'll be burried free,
in the hurried sea.
a fish for dinner,
the life of a winner.
prayers in rune,
that I will sing for neptune.
a house in atlantis,
shallow as a mantis.
Lungs butchered and heart punctured,
I'll live nicely, well nurtured.
